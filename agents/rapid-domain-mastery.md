# Rapid Domain Mastery

Use this when a user needs to understand a new field fast from multiple sources — exam prep, research onboarding, taking over a program, or rapid topic mastery from several documents.

## Core doctrine

Build a field map, not a summary. The goal is an expert's mental model: the core concepts, the live debates, the hidden assumptions, the prerequisites, and the questions that separate real understanding from fake understanding. Stay honest about corpus limits — label gaps rather than papering over them.

## When to use

- Multi-source corpora (papers, docs, handbooks, wiki pages) that must be synthesized.
- Exam/interview prep or a mastery check on a topic.
- Onboarding into a new domain or program.

Not for: plain summaries, single short documents, or quick factual lookups.

## Process

1. **Boundaries first**: decide whether the corpus is strong enough for synthesis. If thin, bound the claims and mark weak spots `[Gap]`.
2. **Input contract**: normalize subject, goal, level, time budget, source shape, and desired mode.
3. **Corpus audit**: inventory the sources, their type, recency, and coverage.
4. **Field cartography + mental models**: map concepts and extract the expert model.
5. **Debate cartography** (when sources disagree): who claims what, and why.
6. **Assumption mining** (every deep run): surface hidden assumptions — where fake understanding hides.
7. **Prerequisite ladder** (when foundations are missing): order what to learn first.
8. **Diagnostic questions** (exam/mastery prep): design oral-exam-style questions that stress understanding.
9. **Tutor loop or study sprint** (on request / time budget): interactive drilling or a dated study plan.
10. **Quality gates** before finalizing; watch failure modes (summary drift, fake consensus, thin-corpus overreach).

## Epistemic labels

Use when helpful: `[Consensus]`, `[Debate]`, `[Open]`, `[Prereq]`, `[Inference]`, `[Gap]`.

## Default output

In this order when appropriate: corpus audit, core mental models, major disagreements, open questions, hidden assumptions, prerequisite ladder, diagnostic questions, and either a tutor start or a sprint plan.

## Canonical phrase

> Map the field, not the page — model the debates and the gaps, not just the consensus.
